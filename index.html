About

Theory group meetings are a place for blackboard talks (slides are welcome, of course). We encourage exploration of all aspects of theoretical astrophysics. Feel free to dive deeply into details of fundamental astrophysical theories. Presentations don’t necessarily have to be your most recent research updates. Paper review or any interesting piece of physics in your mind are also welcome. One meeting usually consists of one or two 15-25min talk + 5-10min discussion + (optional) 5-10min paper review / research update / miscellaneous topics. Let’s have fun!

When and Where

Theory group meetings occur every other Wednesday 11am to 12pm in the Larry Lounge (213) in Astronomy Building.

Spring 2025

  • Feb 5
    • Speaker: Minli Qiu
    • Title: The Search for Planet 9: Evidence, Methods, and Recent Advances
    • Abstract: The hypothesis of Planet Nine, proposed in 2016, suggests the existence of a massive, unseen planet influencing the orbits of trans-Neptunian objects (TNOs). This talk explores the evidence, from gravitational anomalies to observational searches using optical, infrared, and millimeter-wavelength surveys. This talk also reviews recent findings, detection challenges, and alternative explanations like modified gravity. With future telescopes like Vera Rubin Observatory, the search continues, reshaping our understanding of the outer Solar System and its hidden dynamics.

  • Feb 19
    • Speaker: Chase Funkhouser
    • Title: Running Up That Hill: Simulating Outflows from Hot Jupiter Exoplanets
    • Abstract: In 2001 the HST observed the "stereotypical" hot Jupiter exoplanet HD209458b in the Lyman-alpha line, and made an unexpected discovery. The exoplanet's transit was many times deeper in Lyman-alpha than in the optical, meaning the exoplanet must be enveloped by a large cloud of hydrogen extending beyond its Roche Lobe. Since then, many observations of other hot Jupiter and warm sub-Neptune exoplanets have found similar results. These extended hydrogen envelopes reach across the boundary between the exoplanet's atmosphere and the stellar wind and radiation field, making them a great laboratory to study the interactions between stars and their planets. We will discuss the many important pieces of physics at play in this interaction region, and recent particle simulations of the extended envelope of HD209458b.

    • Speaker: Alex Garcia
    • Title: Hey Siri, How Do I Get My Galaxy Simulations to Run Faster?
    • Abstract: Simulations of galaxy formation and evolution are invaluable tools for understanding our Universe. Unfortunately, they are highly expensive. The problem has only gotten worse as higher resolution models become more necessary to keep up with the advent of high resolution observations. In this talk, I will overview the current galaxy simulation paradigm and then introduce some Artificial Intelligence enabled techniques that are presently being used to speed up galaxy simulations. Together, we will weigh the efficiacy of these models against the (potential) huge speed up they can provide.

  • March 5
    • Speaker: Aayush Gautam
    • Title: Paper review: Dynamical heating of newborn stars driven by accretion-induced orbital tightening
    • Abstract: I will review a recent paper on arxiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.18016.

    • Speaker: Chun-Yen Hsu
    • Title: Introduction of the magnetorotational instability
    • Abstract: The magnetorotational instability (MRI) is a fluid instability that causes an accretion disk orbiting a massive central object to become turbulent. In this talk, I will introduce the mechanism of the magnetorotational instability and the implication.
