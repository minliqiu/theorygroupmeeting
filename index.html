About

Theory group meetings are a place for blackboard talks (slides are welcome, of course). We encourage exploration of all aspects of theoretical astrophysics. Feel free to dive deeply into details of fundamental astrophysical theories. Presentations don’t necessarily have to be your most recent research updates. Paper review or any interesting piece of physics in your mind are also welcome. One meeting usually consists of one or two 15-25min talk + 5-10min discussion + (optional) 5-10min paper review / research update / miscellaneous topics. Let’s have fun!

When and Where

Theory group meetings occur every other Wednesday 11am to 12pm in the conference room (201) in Astronomy Building.

  • Sep 27
    • Speaker: Alex Garcia
    • Title: Using Simulated Galaxies to Understand Galaxy Evolution
    • Abstract: In recent decades, simulations of galaxy formation and evolution have become invaluable tools for understanding our Universe. The current simulation landscape encompasses a wide range, from modeling individual galaxies to the large-scale structure of the cosmos, while simultaneously incorporating the physics of stellar feedback, AGN feedback, chemical enrichment, gas accretion, outflows, and more. However, each simulation employs slightly different assumptions, leading to diverse predictions for galaxy properties. Understanding the specific choices made in calibrating these models is essential for distinguishing between different implementations. This talk will investigate the implications of these choices on galaxies, with a particular emphasis on the evolution of metallicity. The metal content of galaxies can trace the gas flows within them, serving as a direct indicator of the underlying physical processes driving galaxy evolution. Our findings suggest that metallicity is a critical area for exploration that may help constrain future simulation models and provide deeper insights into the physics driving galaxy evolution.

    • Speaker: Minli Qiu
    • Title: Introduction to KAM Theory
    • Abstract: KAM Theory (Kolmogorov-Arnold-Moser Theory) examines how nearly integrable Hamiltonian systems behave under small perturbations. It shows that such systems can maintain ordered motion despite disturbances, preventing chaos in certain conditions. The theory connects regular and chaotic dynamics in classical mechanics. This talk will introduce the key mathematical ideas and principles behind KAM theory, offering an overview of its importance in dynamical systems.

  • Nov 8
    • Speaker: Yisheng Tu
    • Title: Single and multiple star formation: modeling through molecular cloud core collapse to the formation of stars and disks
    • Abstract: I will present models of single and multiple star formation through the collapse of molecular cloud cores. Our findings reveal that the protostellar envelope is dominated by structures known as “gravo-magneto-sheetlets,” which arise from the interaction between turbulence, magnetic fields, and gravity. These structures are crucial for transferring mass, angular momentum, and magnetic field from the envelope to the protostellar disk surrounding a forming single star. For the formation of binary or multiple star systems, we propose the “DROD-fragmentation-mechanism” as an alternative to the traditional disk-fragmentation mechanism. Our simulations demonstrate that this mechanism can form misaligned protostellar systems on a 100-au scale in situ.

  • Dec 6
    • Speaker: Devesh Nandal
    • Title: The Role of Rotational Mixing Prescriptions and Magnetic Fields in Massive Star Evolution and Nucleosynthesis
    • Abstract: Stellar rotation and magnetic fields critically influence the evolution and nucleosynthesis of massive stars, impacting their life cycles and the chemical enrichment of galaxies. We examine how different rotational mixing prescriptions and the inclusion of magnetic fields affect 15 M$\odot$ and 60 M$\odot$ stars using the Geneva Stellar Evolution Code across a range of metallicities, including solar, extremely low, and zero metallicity. In nonmagnetic models, the choice of vertical and horizontal diffusion coefficients significantly affects chemical mixing and the evolution of the stellar structure at all metallicities. At solar metallicity, these choices impact the size of the convective core during hydrogen burning and influence whether a star becomes a red or blue supergiant during helium burning. In metal-poor environments, rotational mixing transports carbon and oxygen into the hydrogen-burning shell, activating the CNO cycle and boosting primary nitrogen production-contributing to the nitrogen enrichment observed in high-redshift galaxies. Conversely, models including magnetic fields exhibit suppressed rotational mixing due to magnetic instabilities dominating angular momentum transport, resulting in significantly lower chemical mixing and nitrogen yields across all metallicities. These effects are more pronounced in 15 M$\odot$ stars than in 60 M$\odot$ stars, where mass loss is more influential. Our findings underscore the critical role of rotational mixing prescriptions and magnetic fields in shaping massive star evolution and nucleosynthesis at various metallicities. By comparing our models with observational data, we aim to refine stellar evolution models for better alignment with observed stellar properties and the chemical evolution of galaxies.
